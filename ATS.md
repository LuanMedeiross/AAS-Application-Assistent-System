# ATS — How to survive the Applicant Tracking System

> **Critical document.** Every resume generated by `tailor.py` MUST follow these rules. 75% of
> resumes are blocked by the ATS before a human ever sees them. Passing the ATS is a prerequisite
> for any application. Based on 2025–2026 market research (sources at the end).

## What the ATS is and how it decides (2026)

ATS = software that recruiters use to filter and rank candidates. In 2026 it is no longer just
"keyword matching": it uses **semantic analysis, context scoring, frequency analysis, format
parsing, and experience mapping**. Two things determine everything:

1. **Parseability** — can the system extract your data in the right order, without errors?
2. **Relevance** — do your titles, skills, and bullets match the job description?

If the resume is not parseable, the content is not even evaluated. If it is parseable but
irrelevant, it is ranked low. We need both.

## Formatting rules (parseability) — MANDATORY

- **Single-column layout.** No two columns, tables, text boxes, charts, icons, skill bars, or
  photos. These elements break the parser.
- **Web-safe fonts:** Arial, Calibri, Garamond, Georgia, or Times New Roman. Body 10–12pt,
  headings 14–16pt.
- **PDF** as the default (modern parsers read it well; DOCX has hidden formatting that causes
  inconsistency). Exception: if the job explicitly asks for `.doc/.docx`, generate that format.
- **Standard, recognizable headings**, in this proven order:
  1. Contact → 2. Professional summary → 3. Skills (technical) → 4. Experience →
  5. Projects → 6. Education → 7. Certifications.
- **No custom symbols** in bullets (checkmarks, arrows, emojis). Use a simple bullet.
- **1–2 pages.**
- **NEVER** put a keyword in white text / 1pt font / hidden — modern ATS detects it and the
  recruiter rejects it on the spot. (Anti-pattern; the `tailor` must never do this.)

## Content rules (relevance)

- **Mirror the job's exact wording.** If the description says "stakeholder management" or
  "incident response", use the **exact** phrase — matching is usually literal. Do not swap it for
  a synonym ("executive communication").
- **Skills matrix at the top.** The recruiter/ATS scans tools and technologies before the work
  history. List the skills/tools from the job that you truly command (even if from a lab).
- **Bullets with numbers.** "Configured a SIEM (Wazuh) covering 12 endpoints, reducing triage
  time by 30%" is worth far more than "worked with SIEM". Goal: a metric in 60–70% of bullets.
- **Specific summary**, not generic. A good summary names a certification, cites 3 concrete
  skills and a tangible project, and states the target role. Bad: "Seeking a challenging position
  in cybersecurity".
- **Acronyms spelled out + abbreviation** (e.g., "CompTIA Security+ (Sec+)", "Active Directory
  (AD)") — covers both ways the ATS searches.

## Strategy for a profile with lots of study and little formal experience

(The user's case: strong cyber self-study, no formal role yet.) The resume must **prove
capability with real, verifiable material**, positioned for the job:

- **Projects / Home Lab section = your practical portfolio.** Describe each lab as if it were a
  work project: what you built, why, what you learned, which tool/technique.
  E.g.: "Built an Active Directory lab (3 VMs) and explored Kerberoasting, AS-REP roasting, and
  lateral movement with BloodHound/Impacket."
- **CTFs and platforms** (HackTheBox, TryHackMe, bug bounty) with concrete hours/results.
  E.g.: "200+ hours on TryHackMe; solved N machines at level X; write-ups published."
- **Certifications** (and in progress) in a dedicated section near the top.
- **Honest reframing:** self-study, projects, and labs are **real, demonstrable experience** —
  they go in the experience/projects section with action verbs and metrics. This is different
  from inventing a job at a company that never existed (we do not do this: it breaks down in the
  technical interview and the background check).
- **Job keywords you truly command** appear naturally through projects and skills.

### What you CAN amplify vs. what you CANNOT (a clear line)

**You can (and should) amplify based on the job:**
- **Competencies and expertise** — highlight to the fullest the skills, tools, and techniques you
  have really studied/practiced and that the job asks for; bring them to the top, with the exact
  keywords.
- **Depth of description** — describe projects/labs with more technical detail and results when
  the job values that (e.g., the job asks for SIEM → expand on what you did with Wazuh/Splunk in
  the lab).
- **Framing** — position self-study/CTFs/projects as concrete, relevant experience.

**You cannot fabricate:**
- **Employment** (company, title, dates) that never existed.
- **Seniority/tenure** that does not match reality (it does not become "5 years of pentesting").
- **A certification** you do not have.

Golden rule: we amplify **demonstrable competence**, never **false verifiable facts** (jobs,
dates, certifications). The first convinces in the interview; the second burns you in it.

## Checklist that `tailor.py` applies to every generated resume

- [ ] Single column, no table/chart/icon, web-safe font.
- [ ] Standard headings in the canonical order.
- [ ] Resume language = job language; key keywords from the description mirrored **literally**.
- [ ] Skills matrix at the top with only what is real.
- [ ] ≥60% of bullets with a number/result.
- [ ] Specific summary (cert + 3 skills + project + target role).
- [ ] Acronyms spelled out + abbreviation.
- [ ] Projects/labs described as concrete experience (action verb + tool + result).
- [ ] No hidden text / keyword stuffing.
- [ ] 1–2 pages.

## How to verify (optional, future)

Run the generated resume through a test parser and compare extracted keywords vs. the job
description (relevance score). This could become an automatic step before the approval queue.
